# Sourcing Implications

This note translates the project’s main analytical findings into procurement-style decision questions.

## 1) Broad industry summaries understate sharper component-level concentration

The NAICS-level view suggests only moderate concentration. But the HS6 component-level analysis shows that several electronics categories are much more concentrated than the aggregate picture implies.

**Why this matters:**  
A procurement team can look diversified at the broad category level while still carrying significant exposure in a few critical component families.

**Decision question:**  
Which component categories should be prioritized for second-source qualification, supplier mapping, or contingency planning?

---

## 2) Memory ICs and processors/controllers show especially high concentration

Memory ICs are the most concentrated category in this basket, with Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan accounting for the large majority of import share. Processors/controllers are also concentrated around a narrow supplier base.

**Why this matters:**  
If disruption hits a concentrated category, substitution may be harder, qualification cycles may be longer, and procurement flexibility may be lower than broad sourcing metrics suggest.

**Decision question:**  
Where should buffer inventory, alternate qualification, or deeper supplier-tier visibility be evaluated first?

---

## 3) Country-risk patterns differ by component family

Printed circuits show a different sourcing pattern than integrated circuits, with China playing a larger role in printed circuits while Taiwan dominates several IC categories.

**Why this matters:**  
“Electronics sourcing risk” is not one single problem. Different component families may require different category strategies, different monitoring, and different mitigation plans.

**Decision question:**  
Should category management for electronics be segmented more explicitly by component family rather than treated as one broad sourcing bucket?

---

## 4) A simple scenario lens for procurement teams

If Taiwan share were to decline in a highly concentrated IC category, the key question would not just be where replacement volume exists on paper. The more practical procurement question is whether alternate countries or suppliers are already qualified, commercially viable, and operationally scalable.

This project identifies where concentration risk appears high enough to justify that next layer of sourcing review.
